Quality Education Issue

Global Literacy Among Students

According to United Nations and UNESCO data, millions of children and adolescents worldwide are not achieving basic literacy skills. Approximately 617 million students do not reach minimum proficiency in reading and mathematics. This includes around 387 million primary school-age children and 230 million adolescents of lower secondary school age.

This indicates a serious global learning crisis: being enrolled in school does not guarantee students are learning the foundational skills needed for future success.

Source: United Nations & UNESCO – More than half of children and youth worldwide not learning – UNESCO report

The Digital Divide in Education

Millions of learners worldwide still lack access to home computers, tablets, or reliable internet connections. This digital divide creates significant barriers to education, limiting students’ ability to participate in distance learning, access online resources, and develop essential digital literacy skills. The gap is especially pronounced in low-income countries, rural areas, and communities affected by socio-economic disadvantage. As education increasingly relies on digital tools, addressing this divide is critical to ensuring equitable learning opportunities and achieving the goals of SDG 4.

Source: UNESCO – Startling digital divides in distance learning emerge

Out‑of‑School Children and Youth

Despite decades of progress in expanding access to education, hundreds of millions of children and adolescents remain out of school. According to the UNESCO Global Education Monitoring Report, approximately 251 million children and youth worldwide were not enrolled in school, with the highest rates in low‑income regions and areas affected by conflict and poverty. This persistent exclusion from education highlights ongoing barriers to achieving universal quality education under SDG 4.

Source: UNESCO Global Education Monitoring Report – 251 million children and youth still out of school worldwide

Girls and Gender Inequality in Education

Gender inequality continues to be one of the most persistent barriers to achieving quality education for all. Globally, nearly two-thirds of all illiterate adults are women, highlighting the ongoing disparities in access to education between girls and boys. Socio-economic barriers, cultural norms, and limited opportunities in disadvantaged regions prevent many girls from enrolling in school, completing their education, or developing essential literacy and numeracy skills. Additionally, girls often face higher dropout rates due to early marriage, household responsibilities, or lack of safe and inclusive learning environments, further reinforcing the gender gap in education.

Source: UNESCO – Advancing education and gender equality in and through education

How iamtheCODE Resolves These Issues

IamtheCODE club responds to global education challenges by equipping learners with practical digital and coding skills through inclusive and supportive learning environments. Through community-based programs, collaborative activities, and mentorship initiatives, iamtheCODE works to reduce the digital divide, re-engage out-of-school youth, empower girls and women, and address the learning crisis.

By emphasizing interactive, skills-based education, IamtheCODE ensures that learning leads to real understanding and application. This approach transforms education beyond mere school participation into meaningful opportunities for personal growth, innovation, and long-term success.

Reducing Digital Divide

IamtheCODE club helps reduce the digital divide by lending free laptops and devices to students who do not have access to computers at home. Through its coding clubs and learning programs, learners are able to borrow these devices to practice programming, complete projects, and participate in digital learning activities. This initiative ensures that lack of technology does not prevent students from developing essential coding and digital skills. By providing hands-on access to tools needed for learning, IamtheCODE creates a more inclusive environment where all learners can engage in technology-based education.

Out‑of‑School Children and Youth

Despite improved access to education, some children in nearby communities remain out of school. To address this, the iamtheCODE club adviser at Culiat High School conducts outreach in the Muslim compound to encourage families to enroll their children. This direct engagement helps bring out-of-school youth back into formal education and supports the school’s commitment to inclusive and quality education under SDG 4.

Empowering Women in Education and Technology

IamtheCODE club addresses gender inequality by actively empowering girls and women through inclusive and supportive technology education. By creating safe learning spaces, encouraging participation in coding activities, and providing mentorship opportunities, the program helps women build confidence and develop essential digital skills. IamtheCODE also challenges traditional barriers and stereotypes that limit access to education by promoting equal opportunities for learning and leadership. Through skills development and peer support, women are empowered to participate fully in education and pursue opportunities in technology and beyond.

Learning Through Collaboration

IamtheCODE club addresses the learning crisis by promoting collaborative and interactive learning environments where students actively engage with one another. Through group programming activities, peer to peer teaching, and teamwork-based projects, learners are encouraged to explain concepts, share ideas, and solve problems together. This approach strengthens understanding, improves retention of knowledge, and builds confidence in applying skills. By shifting learning from passive instruction to active participation, IamtheCODE ensures that students do not simply attend sessions, but genuinely learn and develop essential problem-solving and digital skills.
